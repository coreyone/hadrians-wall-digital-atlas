# PRINCIPLE I: STRUCTURAL INTEGRITY (The Architecture of Writing)
## The Paragraph as the Unit
1.  Make the paragraph the unit of composition.
2.  Adhere to a suitable design and hold to it.
3.  Foresee or determine the shape of what is to come and pursue that shape.
4.  Use single paragraphs for brief descriptions, book reviews, or single ideas.
5.  Divide subjects of significant extent into topics, treating each in a separate paragraph.
6.  Begin each paragraph with a sentence that suggests the topic or aids transition.
7.  End paragraphs with a signal that a new step in development has been reached.
8.  In dialogue, start a new paragraph with each change of speaker.
9.  Avoid writing single sentences as paragraphs unless they are transitional.
10. Break long paragraphs in two to provide visual help to the reader.
11. Avoid firing off many short paragraphs in quick succession to prevent distraction.
12. Use moderation and a sense of order as the main considerations in paragraphing.

## Sentence Construction
13. Do not break sentences in two; do not use periods for commas.
14. Avoid a succession of loose sentences consisting of two clauses.
15. Replace monotonous loose sentences with simple sentences or those joined by semicolons.
16. Use the active voice; it is more direct and vigorous than the passive.
17. Put statements in positive form; avoid noncommittal language.
18. Use the word "not" for denial or antithesis, never for evasion.
19. Express coordinate ideas in similar form (parallel construction).
20. Repeat an article or preposition before each term in a series or only before the first.
21. Keep related words together to show their relationship.
22. Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end.
23. Place the subject of a sentence at the beginning for emphasis only if it is also the predicate.
24. Avoid separating the subject and principal verb by a phrase that can be moved.
25. Place the relative pronoun immediately after its antecedent.
26. If an antecedent is a group of words, place the relative at the end of the group.
27. Place modifiers next to the words they modify.
28. In summaries, keep to one tense.
29. Use the present tense when summarizing the action of a drama.

# PRINCIPLE II: ECONOMY & BREVITY (The Removal of Noise)
## Omission of the Superfluous
30. Vigorous writing is concise.
31. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words.
32. A paragraph should contain no unnecessary sentences.
33. Make every word tell.
34. Revise the expression "the fact that" out of every sentence.
35. Eliminate "who is" and "which was" when they are superfluous.
36. Simplify "the question as to whether" to "whether".
37. Simplify "there is no doubt but that" to "no doubt" or "doubtless".
38. Simplify "used for fuel purposes" to "used for fuel".
39. Simplify "in a hasty manner" to "hastily".
40. Simplify "this is a subject that" to "this subject".
41. Simplify "his strange story" instead of "his story is a strange one".
42. Avoid the expression "the reason why is that"; use "because".
43. Combine a series of short sentences into one complex sentence to avoid wordiness.
44. Do not overstate; it causes the reader to lose confidence.
45. Avoid the use of qualifiers like "rather," "very," "little," and "pretty".
46. Do not affect a breezy manner; it often indicates emptiness.
47. Do not explain too much in dialogue tags.
48. Avoid fancy words; do not be tempted by a twenty-dollar word when a ten-center will do.
49. Do not take shortcuts at the cost of clarity.
50. Avoid foreign languages unless convenient or necessary.

# PRINCIPLE III: CLARITY & PRECISION (The Transfer of Meaning)
## Specificity
51. Use definite, specific, concrete language.
52. Prefer the specific to the general.
53. Prefer the definite to the vague.
54. Prefer the concrete to the abstract.
55. Arouse and hold attention by dealing in particulars.
56. Report the details that matter.
57. In exposition and argument, furnish particular instances of general principles.
58. Make sure the reader knows who is speaking in dialogue.
59. Be clear; clarity is a virtue in communication.
60. Break apart sentences that have become too involved.
61. Do not inject opinion unless there is a good reason.
62. Use figures of speech sparingly.
63. Do not mix metaphors.
64. Write out names of organizations fully before using initials.
65. Prefer the standard to the offbeat.
66. Avoid the language of advertising and mutilation of standard English.
67. Be suspicious of business vocabulary (e.g., "finalize").
68. Write with nouns and verbs, not adjectives and adverbs.

## Ambiguity Avoidance
69. Ensure "this" refers clearly to a specific antecedent, not a general idea.
70. Distinguish "farther" (distance) from "further" (time/quantity).
71. Distinguish "imply" (suggest) from "infer" (deduce).
72. Distinguish "anticipate" from "expect".
73. Distinguish "uninterested" (not interested) from "disinterested" (impartial).
74. Distinguish "less" (quantity) from "fewer" (number).
75. Distinguish "tortuous" (winding) from "torturous" (painful).
76. Distinguish "verbal" (word for word) from "oral" (spoken).
77. Restrict "while" to "during the time that" to avoid ambiguity with "although" or "and".
78. Avoid using "thanking you in advance".
79. Do not use "they" with distributive expressions like "each" or "every one".

# PRINCIPLE IV: MECHANICS & SYNTAX (The Rules of the Road)
## Punctuation
80. Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding 's.
81. Follow the possessive rule whatever the final consonant (e.g., Charles's).
82. Exceptions to possessive: ancient proper names in -es, -is (e.g., Jesus').
83. Pronominal possessives (hers, its, theirs, yours, ours) have no apostrophe.
84. Indefinite pronouns use the apostrophe to show possession (one's rights).
85. Do not confuse "it's" (it is) with "its" (possessive).
86. In a series of three or more terms with a single conjunction, use a comma after each term except the last (serial comma).
87. Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas.
88. Never omit one comma and leave the other in a parenthetic expression.
89. Place a comma before a conjunction introducing an independent clause.
90. Do not join independent clauses with a comma (comma splice).
91. Use a semicolon to join independent clauses not joined by a conjunction.
92. Use a semicolon if the second clause is preceded by an adverb like "accordingly".
93. Use a colon after an independent clause to introduce a list, appositive, or amplification.
94. A colon should not separate a verb from its complement or a preposition from its object.
95. Use a dash to set off an abrupt break or interruption.
96. Use a dash to announce a long appositive or summary.
97. Use a dash only when a more common mark of punctuation is inadequate.
98. Do not use a hyphen between words that can be written as one word.
99. Use a hyphen when two or more words are combined to form a compound adjective.
100. Punctuate parenthetical sentences within parentheses exactly as if they stood alone, omitting the final stop unless it is a question/exclamation.
101. Place the comma inside quotation marks.
102. Place the colon outside quotation marks.
103. Do not use quotation marks for colloquialisms; simply use the word.

## Grammar & Agreement
104. The number of the subject determines the number of the verb.
105. Words intervening between subject and verb do not affect the number of the verb.
106. Use a singular verb after "each," "either," "everyone," "everybody," "neither," "nobody," "someone".
107. With "none," use singular when it means "no one"; use plural when it suggests more than one.
108. A compound subject joined by "and" almost always requires a plural verb.
109. A singular subject remains singular even if connected to other nouns by "with," "as well as," etc..
110. A linking verb agrees with the number of its subject.
111. "The contents" of a book is singular; "contents" of a jar can be plural.
112. Use the proper case of pronoun (subject vs. object).
113. A pronoun in a comparison is nominative if it is the subject of an understood verb (e.g., "better than I").
114. Use the simple personal pronoun as a subject, not "myself" or "yourself".
115. Gerunds usually require the possessive case (e.g., "my asking").
116. A present participle as a verbal takes the objective case.
117. A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to the grammatical subject.
118. Avoid dangling participles.
119. In formal writing, use "shall" for the first person, "will" for second/third (future tense).
120. "Will" in the first person expresses determination or consent.

# PRINCIPLE V: USAGE & WORD CHOICE (The Tactical Corrections)
## Commonly Misused Words (A-E)
121. Aggravate: Means "to add to," not "to irritate".
122. All right: Write as two words.
123. Allude: Do not confuse with "refer"; allusion is indirect.
124. Allusion: Do not confuse with "illusion" (unreal image).
125. Alternate: Every other one; distinct from "alternative" (choice).
126. Among: Use when more than two are involved.
127. Between: Use when more than two are involved but considered individually.
128. And/or: Avoid this device; it damages sentences.
129. Anticipate: Use "expect" for simple expectation.
130. Anybody: Write as one word for "any person".
131. Anyone: Write as one word for "anybody".
132. As good or better than: Rearrange to "My opinion is as good as his, or better".
133. As to whether: "Whether" is sufficient.
134. As yet: "Yet" is nearly always better.
135. Being: Not appropriate after "regarded as".
136. But: Unnecessary after "doubt" and "help".
137. Can: Means "able"; do not use as a substitute for "may".
138. Care less: "I could care less" destroys the meaning; use "I couldn't care less".
139. Case: Often unnecessary; "in many cases" -> "many".
140. Certainly: Avoid indiscriminate use to intensify statements.
141. Character: Often redundant (e.g., "acts of a hostile character").
142. Claim: Means "lay claim to," not "declare" or "maintain".
143. Clever: Means good-natured when applied to horses; distinct from ingenious.
144. Compare to: Point out resemblances between different orders.
145. Compare with: Point out differences between same orders.
146. Comprise: Literally "embrace"; the whole comprises the parts.
147. Consider: Not followed by "as" when it means "believe to be".
148. Contact: Vague; use "get in touch," "phone," "meet".
149. Cope: Use "cope with"; do not use intransitively.
150. Currently: Usually redundant with present tense verbs.
151. Data: Best used as a plural (e.g., "these data are").
152. Different than: Use "different from".
153. Divided into: Do not misuse for "composed of".
154. Due to: Loosely used for "because of"; correct when synonymous with "attributable to".
155. Each and every one: Avoid; pitchman's jargon.
156. Effect: Noun means result; verb means to bring about.
157. Enormity: Means "monstrous wickedness," not bigness.
158. Enthuse: Not recommended; use "enthusiastic".
159. Etc.: Equivalent to "and the rest"; incorrect at end of list introduced by "such as".

## Commonly Misused Words (F-O)
160. Fact: Use only for matters capable of direct verification.
161. Facility: Avoid vague use for jails, hospitals, schools.
162. Factor: Hackneyed; replace with direct phrasing.
163. Feature: Hackneyed; usually adds nothing.
164. Finalize: Pompous and ambiguous.
165. Fix: Colloquial for arrange/mend; preferred meaning is "to make firm".
166. Flammable: "Inflammable" is the common word for combustible.
167. Folk: Use singular "folk"; "folks" is too folksy.
168. Fortuitous: Limited to what happens by chance; not "fortunate".
169. Get: Prefer "got" to "gotten" as participle.
170. Gratuitous: Means unearned or unwarranted.
171. He is a man who: Redundant; simply say "He".
172. Hopefully: Do not use to mean "I hope"; means "with hope".
173. However: Avoid starting a sentence with "however" when meaning "nevertheless".
174. Imply/Infer: Speaker implies; listener infers.
175. Importantly: Avoid; use "important" or rephrase.
176. In regard to: "In regards to" is wrong; use "in regard to" or "as regards".
177. In the last analysis: Bankrupt expression.
178. Inside of: "Of" is unnecessary unless meaning "in less than".
179. Insightful: Suspicious overstatement for "perceptive".
180. In terms of: Padding; usually omit.
181. Interesting: Avoid as an introduction; make the story interesting instead.
182. Irregardless: Should be "regardless".
183. -ize: Do not coin verbs by adding -ize (e.g., prioritize).
184. Kind of: Do not use as substitute for "rather"; restrict to literal sense.
185. Lay: Transitive verb; do not misuse for "lie" (intransitive).
186. Leave: Do not misuse for "let".
187. Less: Refers to quantity; "fewer" refers to number.
188. Like: Governs nouns/pronouns; use "as" before phrases/clauses.
189. Line: "Along these lines" is overworked; discard it.
190. Literal/Literally: Do not use to support exaggeration.
191. Loan: Noun; use "lend" for the verb.
192. Meaningful: Bankrupt adjective; choose another.
193. Memento: Not "momento".
194. Most: Do not use for "almost" in formal composition.
195. Nature: Often redundant (e.g., "acts of a hostile nature").
196. Nauseous: Means "sickening to contemplate"; "nauseated" means sick at stomach.
197. Nice: Shaggy, all-purpose word; use sparingly.
198. Nor: Often used wrongly for "or" after negative expressions.
199. Noun used as verb: Suspect usage (e.g., "gift," "host," "chair").
200. Offputting/Ongoing: Inexact and clumsy; avoid.
201. One: Not to be followed by "his" or "her"; use "one's".
202. One of the most: Feeble formula; avoid.
203. -oriented: Clumsy; find a better way to indicate direction.

## Commonly Misused Words (P-Z)
204. Partially: Not always interchangeable with "partly"; "partly" implies distinct part.
205. Participle for verbal noun: Use possessive with gerund (e.g., "Senate's accepting").
206. People: Political term; distinct from "public"; best not used with numbers.
207. Personalize: Pretentious; do not "personalize" prose.
208. Personally: Often unnecessary.
209. Possess: Often used for impressiveness; "have" or "own" is better.
210. Presently: Ambiguous; restrict to "in a short time" (not currently).
211. Prestigious: Adjective of last resort.
212. Regretful: Sometimes carelessly used for "regrettable".
213. Relate: Do not use intransitively to suggest rapport.
214. Respective/Respectively: Usually omit with advantage.
215. Secondly/Thirdly: Modern usage prefers "second," "third".
216. So: Avoid use as an intensifier ("so good").
217. Split infinitive: Avoid unless necessary to place unusual stress.
218. State: Restrict to "express fully/clearly"; not a substitute for "say".
219. Student body: Needless; use "students".
220. Than: Examine sentences to ensure no essential words are missing.
221. That: Restrictive pronoun (tells which one).
222. Which: Nonrestrictive pronoun (adds a fact); "go which-hunting".
223. The foreseeable future: Fuzzy clich√©.
224. The truth is: Bad beginning for a sentence.
225. They: Do not use with singular antecedents like "everybody".
226. This: Can produce imprecise statements if referring to a whole clause.
227. Thrust: Showy noun; use sparingly.
228. Transpire: Means "become known," not "happen".
229. Try: Takes the infinitive ("try to"), not "try and".
230. Type: Not a synonym for "kind of" (e.g., "that type of employee").
231. Unique: Means "without like or equal"; cannot be "very unique".
232. Utilize: Prefer "use".
233. Very: Use sparingly; use stronger words instead.
234. While: Avoid indiscriminate use for "and," "but," "although".
235. -wise: Do not use as a pseudo-suffix (e.g., "taxwise").
236. Worth while: Strictly applicable only to actions.
237. Worthwhile: Adjective is acceptable but emaciated.
238. Would: Omit when expressing habitual action; use past tense.

# PRINCIPLE VI: ATTITUDE & APPROACH (The Writer's Mindset)
## Naturalness & Tone
239. Place yourself in the background.
240. Draw attention to the sense and substance, not the mood of the author.
241. Write in a way that comes naturally.
242. Use words and phrases that come readily to hand.
243. Never imitate consciously, but admire what is good.
244. Work from a suitable design; plan before writing.
245. Do not overwrite; rich, ornate prose is hard to digest.
246. Do not construct awkward adverbs (e.g., "tangledly").
247. Do not use dialect unless your ear is good.
248. Write primarily to please and satisfy yourself.
249. Writing is an act of faith, not a trick of grammar.
250. Believe in the truth and worth of the scrawl.
